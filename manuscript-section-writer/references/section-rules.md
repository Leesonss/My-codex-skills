# Section Rules

## All Sections

Use a clear claim-to-evidence-to-reasoning sequence. Keep statements at the strength supported by their evidence. Maintain approved terminology and avoid literature-by-literature narration. Distinguish prior findings from the authors' inference and research proposal.

## Introduction

Move from the focal phenomenon and theoretical problem to what is known, the precise unresolved issue, the study's approach, and two or three defensible contributions. Make each contribution correspond to a gap, model element, and study capability. Do not substitute broad context for a theoretical problem, treat "few studies" as a gap, claim to be first without suitable verification, turn the Introduction into a literature review, or present practical relevance as theoretical contribution.

## Literature Review Or Theoretical Background

Organize around questions rather than author chronology: construct definition and distinction, core theory, evidence for focal relationships, contradictions or limits, and the foundation required by the study. Do not repeat an evidence matrix; synthesize it into a directed argument. State only what the selected corpus supports.

## Hypotheses Development

For each hypothesis: define the relevant constructs, state the theory premise, explain the proposed mechanism, use relevant empirical evidence, state a contextual boundary where applicable, acknowledge a material alternative when it changes the inference, then give a concise and testable hypothesis sentence.

For mediation, distinguish `X -> M`, `M -> Y`, and the indirect effect. For moderation, state the moderated path, the directional mechanism, and what differs at high versus low levels. Do not imply that past research tested the exact full model when it supports only segments. Avoid repeating shared arguments across hypotheses.

## Theoretical Contributions

For each contribution, state the previous limit, what the study adds, revises, or clarifies, the result or supported proposition that bears on it, and whether it changes explanation, prediction, or scope. Classify it accurately as application, extension, refinement, integration, challenge, construct, empirical, or contextual contribution. Retain only the strongest two or three. Do not write "fills a gap," "extends prior research," or "provides insights" without specifying the change.

## Practical Implications

Specify the affected decision-maker, context, action, mechanism, risk, and boundary. Derive each implication from the model and actual results. Do not promote unmeasured behavior, offer unrelated advice, omit negative consequences, or present a generic recommendation as a study-specific result.

## Discussion

Before results, draft only an outline, alternative-result scenarios, possible interpretations, and conditional implications. Never write a final Discussion as though results are known.

After results, organize around principal findings, agreement or disagreement with hypotheses, explanation of supported and unsupported paths, comparison with literature, theory implications, alternatives and boundary conditions, practical implications, and limitations. Do not ignore null results, recast exploratory findings as predicted, generalize beyond the design, or say one study proves a theory.

## Limitations And Future Research

For every limitation, specify the limitation, its likely effect on interpretation, the conclusion that remains defensible, and an actionable future-study response. Derive future research from unresolved mechanisms, boundaries, contradictions, or design constraints rather than generic calls for more research.

## Abstract

Write a final abstract only when the design and principal results are known. Cover research question, theory, method, central results, contribution, and only necessary practical implications. Without results, produce a clearly marked proposal abstract or placeholder version. Never create a sample, study count, effect size, or result.
