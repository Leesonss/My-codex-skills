# Gap And Contribution Framework

Use this framework after completing the nearest-neighbor and construct-overlap searches. Apply more than one gap category when justified, but avoid multiplying labels without distinct evidence.

## Gap Categories

### Theoretical Gap

Use when an important phenomenon remains unexplained, theories make conflicting predictions, or a core theoretical assumption or boundary requires revision. A strong theoretical gap changes explanation, prediction, assumptions, or scope.

### Mechanism Gap

Use when a relationship is established but competing processes imply meaningfully different explanations or predictions. Adding a mediator is insufficient. Explain why the mechanism matters, which alternatives it excludes, and what discriminating evidence is possible.

### Boundary-Condition Gap

Use when a theoretically specified individual, technology, task, context, culture, or time condition changes an effect. Adding an unused moderator is insufficient. Explain why the moderator should change direction, magnitude, or process.

### Construct Gap

Use when existing constructs fail to describe the phenomenon or conflate distinct properties. Require a clear definition, distinctive attributes, differentiable antecedents or outcomes, a nonredundant measure or manipulation, and incremental explanatory value.

### Contradictory-Evidence Gap

Use for opposing, mixed, null, method-dependent, or context-dependent findings. First test whether design, sample, measurement, power, or operationalization already explains the inconsistency.

### Methodological Gap

Use when conclusions depend heavily on cross-sectional designs, self-report, student samples, one culture, one stimulus, one measure, correlational evidence, or no behavioral outcome. Treat this primarily as empirical or robustness value unless it changes a theoretical inference.

### Contextual Gap

Use when a setting, industry, country, population, or technology is understudied. Treat the contribution as theoretically stronger only when the context changes a mechanism, assumption, effect direction, boundary, construct meaning, or consequence.

## Weak-Gap Warnings

Downgrade a gap based only on:

- `few studies have examined` or a small retrieved count;
- a new country, industry, platform, technology, or sample;
- the first combination of familiar variables in one model;
- adding one mediator or moderator;
- repeating an established relationship with a new method;
- no match among the first search results or no identical paper title;
- relabeling an existing construct;
- a more complex model without greater explanatory value;
- practical importance without a theoretical problem;
- an untested direct effect presented as missing knowledge;
- confusing no direct study with no relevant knowledge.

Never infer `first study`, `no research exists`, `never examined`, or `ignored by the literature` from a closed-corpus retrieval miss.

## Nearest-Neighbor Analysis

Search for the same independent variable, dependent variable, mediator, moderator, theory, setting, technology, interaction, adjacent construct, functionally equivalent construct, research question, and model structure. Search relevant upstream and downstream literatures.

Build one row per distinct source or supported study with:

- citation handle and source ID;
- title and year;
- research question;
- theory;
- focal constructs and relationships;
- context and method;
- key finding;
- similarity to the proposed study;
- substantive difference;
- novelty threat: high, medium, or low;
- residual incremental value;
- evidence label and locator;
- further verification needed.

Do not treat wording differences as substantive novelty. Rank the most threatening neighbors first.

## Construct Distinctiveness

For every new, renamed, or central ambiguous construct, compare it with adjacent constructs on:

1. definition;
2. core attributes;
3. theoretical foundation;
4. antecedents;
5. outcomes;
6. measurement;
7. manipulation;
8. applicable context;
9. noninterchangeable content;
10. incremental explanatory value.

Rate distinctiveness as `strong`, `moderate`, `weak`, or `not verified`. When weak, recommend renaming, merging, narrowing the definition, strengthening boundaries, repositioning the construct, testing discriminant and incremental validity, or dropping the new-construct claim.

## Contribution Categories

- `theory application`: apply an established theory without changing it materially.
- `theory extension`: add a mechanism, boundary, relation, construct, or scope that creates new predictions.
- `theory refinement`: clarify or correct an imprecise or overbroad assumption.
- `theory integration`: combine theories to produce an explanation or prediction unavailable from either alone.
- `theory challenge`: document a theoretically diagnostic conflict and propose a correction or alternative.
- `construct contribution`: introduce, distinguish, redefine, or reposition a construct with independent explanatory value.
- `empirical contribution`: add data, methods, samples, contexts, or robustness evidence with limited theoretical change.
- `contextual contribution`: show how a context changes mechanisms, predictions, boundaries, or construct meaning.
- `practical contribution`: improve a managerial, consumer, technical, or social decision; assess separately from theory.

For each claimed contribution answer:

1. What understanding changes?
2. What would current theory predict wrongly, fail to predict, or omit?
3. Does the study create a new explanation or prediction?
4. Does it establish a theoretically meaningful boundary?
5. Does it distinguish confusable constructs or explain contradictory evidence?
6. Is it only an application in a new setting?
7. Does the claim exceed the proposed design or data?
8. Has a nearest neighbor already made the same contribution?
9. Would a reviewer see it as incremental only?

Rate each contribution:

- `strongly supported`;
- `moderately supported`;
- `plausible but requires verification`;
- `weak`;
- `overstated`;
- `unsupported`.

## Reviewer 2 Pressure Test

Raise at least these objections:

1. strongest objection to the gap;
2. strongest objection to novelty;
3. strongest objection to construct distinctiveness;
4. strongest objection to theory choice;
5. rival explanation for the mediator;
6. necessity of the moderator;
7. whether context adds theory rather than setting;
8. causal-identification weakness;
9. overstatement of practical value;
10. likely desk-rejection issue.

For each objection state whether it is valid, severity, evidence needed, whether writing alone can resolve it, whether literature, model, or design changes are required, and a defensible revision to the claim.

## Verdict Logic

- `Proceed`: a defensible gap survives nearest-neighbor, overlap, theory, and design checks with manageable uncertainty.
- `Proceed after revision`: the core idea survives but claims, variables, theory, or positioning require bounded changes.
- `Major redesign required`: the phenomenon may matter, but the current model or contribution logic does not survive major objections.
- `Current evidence insufficient`: the available RAG evidence cannot support a reliable judgment.
- `Do not proceed with the current model`: nearest neighbors, overlap, or design constraints leave no defensible incremental value without replacing the model.
