# Audit Rules

## Claim and evidence types

Use the type that controls the evidence standard: definition, theoretical, empirical association, causal, mediation, moderation, consensus, contradiction, methodological, novelty, practical, measurement, or interpretation.

Separate theoretical propositions, conceptual explanations, empirical results, review trends, meta-analytic effects, author Discussion interpretations, and Future Research suggestions. Never turn a theory proposition into an established empirical fact, a Discussion explanation into a result, or a Future Research suggestion into a finding.

## A-E support grades

Apply these grades systematically only to critical claims in Standard and Forensic modes:

- **A - Directly supported**: construct, relationship, direction, evidence type, sample, and setting match the claim.
- **B - Supported through synthesis**: several sources jointly support the claim. State the contribution of each source and how the synthesis was formed.
- **C - Reasonable inference**: the evidence permits a reasoned deduction, but the source did not directly state or test it. Accept when the text clearly uses language such as `we argue`, `we propose`, `may`, `might`, or `is likely to`.
- **D - Weak or overstated**: related evidence exists but wording is too strong, scope is expanded, a condition is made universal, a correlation is made causal, or one study is made into consensus.
- **E - Unsupported or mismatched**: the source does not study the relation, direction conflicts, the key is wrong, the claim cannot be verified, or a critical claim lacks usable evidence.

B and C are not errors by themselves. Classify the problem as citation error, wording calibration, acceptable theoretical inference, evidence limitation, or optional improvement.

## High-risk checks

- Causal wording (`cause`, `lead to`, `result in`, `increase`, `reduce`, `drive`, `determine`) requires design and temporal support. With cross-sectional, correlational, or self-report evidence, consider calibrated wording such as `is associated with`, `relates to`, `is linked to`, `predicts`, or `may influence`.
- A mediation claim needs evidence for X to M, M to Y, the indirect effect, temporal order, and relevant alternatives. Evidence for only one segment is partial support.
- A moderation claim needs an interaction, its direction, significance or uncertainty, and the boundary conditions. A theory that predicts a moderator is not the same as an empirical test of it.
- Check scope across country, culture, industry, technology, population, task, time, construct, and outcome. Do not turn one sample into a universal law.
- Strong consensus wording (`well established`, `research consistently shows`, `scholars generally agree`) requires multiple independent studies, reasonably consistent directions, varied samples or methods, and no material ignored contradiction.
- Actively search counterevidence only for core hypotheses, strong consensus or causal claims, mediation or moderation, novelty or contribution claims, material Discussion interpretations, or an explicit Forensic audit.
- For novelty, check synonyms, adjacent constructs, and prior synthesis. Never infer `first study` or `no prior research` from a failed search alone.

## Recommended dispositions

For C, D, or E critical claims choose one or more: retain; calibrate wording; split the claim; add a boundary; add or replace a citation; remove a citation; delete the claim; recast as an inference; or mark for further verification. Prefer a directly replaceable academic sentence when the evidence permits it.
